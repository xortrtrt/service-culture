Module 4

The Power of Empathy

Empathy is the capacity to recognise what another person is experiencing and to respond in a way that takes that experience seriously. Researchers typically split it into three components, and each one shows up differently on a service floor.

Cognitive empathy

Understanding the other person’s point of view intellectually. The agent who reads a frustrated email and thinks, “They’ve been bounced between three teams; they need closure.”

Affective empathy

Feeling something of what the other person feels. Useful for warmth, but unmanaged it leads to absorption and burnout.

Compassionate empathy

Understanding plus feeling plus the impulse to help. This is the version that shows up in CSAT verbatims.

Common empathy blockers

  • Premature problem-solving — jumping to a fix before the customer feels heard.
  • Defensive listening — treating the complaint as an attack on you or the brand.
  • Comparison — “other customers have it worse” minimises the person in front of you.
  • Script overrun — reading the next line instead of the next person.

“Empathy is placing one’s self in the shoes of another — long enough to walk a step, not so long that you forget you have your own shoes to put back on.”

Module 5

Social Skills & Conflict

Conflict in a service setting is not failure; it is information. The Thomas–Kilmann instrument identifies five conflict-handling styles — competing, collaborating, compromising, avoiding, and accommodating — and most professionals over-use one of them under stress. The skill is not picking a single “best” style but matching the style to the stakes and the relationship.

Collaborating

High concern for both sides. Use when the relationship and outcome both matter (key account renewals, cross-team SLA design).

Compromising

Quick, partial wins for both. Use under time pressure when collaborating is too expensive.

Accommodating

Yielding to preserve goodwill. Useful when you are wrong, or when the issue matters far more to the other party.

Avoiding / Competing

Avoid for trivial issues; compete only when a quick decision is required and you are accountable for it.

Module 6

The GROW Coaching Model

Sir John Whitmore popularised the GROW model in the 1980s as a way to structure a coaching conversation in roughly thirty minutes. Its discipline is that the coach asks rather than tells: the coachee leaves the session with a plan they generated themselves and are therefore far more likely to execute.

Use the worksheet below to run a real session — either on yourself or with a teammate — and print the result as a one-page commitment.

G

Goal

What does success look like for this session?

›

What outcome do you want? How will you know you have achieved it? Is the goal in your control?

R

Reality

Examine the current situation without judgment.

›

What is happening now? What have you already tried? Who else is affected? What is the cost of doing nothing?

O

Options

Brainstorm possible paths forward.

›

What could you do? What else? If resources were unlimited, what would you try? Which option excites you most, and why?

W

Will

Commit to a specific action plan.

›

What will you do, by when, and who will hold you accountable? On a scale of 1–10, how committed are you? If under 8, what would raise it?
