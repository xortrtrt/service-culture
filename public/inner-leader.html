Why begin with the self?

Daniel Goleman's research on emotional intelligence is unambiguous on one point: every interpersonal skill that distinguishes high-performing leaders — empathy, conflict management, persuasion, coaching — is built on a foundation of accurate self-assessment. Leaders who cannot name what they feel, what they want, and how they are perceived consistently misread the rooms they walk into.

Pillar 1 develops that foundation in three layers: a model for mapping what you know and don't know about yourself (Module 1), a framework for identifying what actually drives your behaviour (Module 2), and a writing exercise that converts both into a personal mission and vision (Module 3).

The Johari Window

Joseph Luft and Harrington Ingham introduced the Johari Window in 1955 as a simple 2×2 grid that crosses two axes: what is known to the self and what is known to others. The four resulting panes — Open, Blind, Hidden, and Unknown — describe every piece of information about you that exists in a working relationship.

The model is useful because it reframes self-awareness from a vague aspiration into two concrete behaviours. You enlarge the Open pane by asking for feedback (which shrinks the Blind area) and by appropriate self-disclosure (which shrinks the Hidden area). Both behaviours are uncomfortable; both compound trust on a team faster than any culture-deck slogan.

In a service environment this matters operationally. A team leader with a large Blind pane will keep escalating the same coaching message that agents have already privately rejected. An agent with a large Hidden pane will absorb conflict with a customer until burnout forces an unplanned absence. Both failure modes are predictable from the window.

Module 2

Drivers of Motivation

A leader’s primary operational task is to keep capable people energised long enough to deliver. That requires a sharper vocabulary than “morale.” Two complementary frameworks — Frederick Herzberg’s Two-Factor Theory and David McClelland’s Theory of Needs — do most of the heavy lifting.

Herzberg: hygiene vs. motivators

Herzberg distinguished hygiene factors (salary, working conditions, policies, supervision) from motivators (achievement, recognition, the work itself, growth).

The asymmetry is the point: removing a hygiene problem only takes someone from dissatisfied to neutral. Genuine engagement comes from motivators. A pay rise stops complaints; it does not buy discretionary effort.

McClelland: three acquired needs

McClelland argued that adults are dominated by one of three learned needs:

  • Achievement (nAch): mastering challenging, measurable goals.
  • Affiliation (nAff): harmonious relationships and belonging.
  • Power / Authority (nPow): influence over outcomes and people.

Practical synthesis: use Herzberg to remove friction (fix the schedule, fix the tooling, stop micro-managing) and use McClelland to assign work (hand the toughest queue to the achievement-driven agent, the buddy programme to the affiliation-driven agent, the project lead role to the power-driven agent). Misalignment between need and assignment is one of the most common — and most fixable — sources of attrition in the floor.

Module 3

Personal Vision & Mission

A mission describes what you do today and the standard you hold yourself to while doing it; it answers the question, “If a colleague described my work this quarter, what would I want them to be able to say?” A vision describes the future state your present work is in service of; it answers, “If I keep operating at this standard for five years, where do I land?”

Written well, the two statements act as a personal filter. New opportunities can be evaluated against them in seconds: this assignment moves me toward the vision, that one does not. Without them, every new request looks equally urgent, which is how careers drift.

Mission — the present

Defines your purpose and the standard you hold today.

“To deliver excellent service by ensuring prompt assistance, technical accuracy, and a tone that makes every customer feel taken seriously.”

Vision — the future

Extends the mission to an ideal future state.

“To become a recognised leader in the IT‑BPM sector who is trusted to bridge technology and human connection across cultures.”

A drafting checklist

  • Write in the first person and the present tense, even for the vision.
  • Name the standard, not just the activity (“deliver clear technical answers” beats “answer tickets”).
  • Keep each statement under thirty words. If it doesn’t fit on a sticky note, you won’t use it.
  • Re-read it every Monday morning for a month. Edit until it stops feeling aspirational and starts feeling accurate.
